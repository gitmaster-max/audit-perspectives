# Audit Perspectives

A multi-segment, role-differentiated method for understanding how any audit question looks across every layer of an engagement team — from the Staff auditor executing procedures to the PCAOB inspector reviewing the file years later.

## Why this exists

A single view of an audit question returns the answer from whoever you asked. A Partner and a Staff auditor on the same engagement are looking at the same risk with completely different information, incentives, and accountability. IT Audit brings control-reliance judgment that Core Audit often underweights. Tax sees deferred tax and provision implications before Core Audit has asked the question. Advisory sees process risk that predates the audit. External stakeholders — regulators, inspectors, financial users — are evaluating the same work product against criteria the engagement team never sees in real time.

This skill surfaces all of those views simultaneously, maps where they conflict, and synthesizes a complete picture of what an audit question actually means across the full ecosystem.

Stanford's STORM (Shao et al., NAACL 2024) demonstrated that multi-perspective questioning produces materially broader, more organized analysis than single-pass research. This skill applies the same logic to the audit profession: the blind spots of any one role get caught by another, and the intersections are where the real risk lives.

## Segments

### Internal

| Segment | Roles |
|---|---|
| **Core Audit** | Staff, Senior, Manager, Senior Manager, Executive Director, Partner, Independent Reviewer, Quality Reviewer (EQCR) |
| **IT Audit** | Staff, Senior, Manager, Senior Manager, Executive Director, Partner |
| **Tax** | Staff, Senior, Manager, Senior Manager, Executive Director, Partner |
| **Advisory** | Staff, Senior, Manager, Senior Manager, Executive Director, Partner |

### External

| Stakeholder | What they evaluate |
|---|---|
| **PCAOB Inspection** | Whether audit work meets PCAOB AS standards; deficiency risk; root cause of failures |
| **AICPA Peer Review** | GAAS compliance; methodology application for non-PCAOB engagements |
| **End Financial Users** | Whether financial statements are reliable; whether disclosures are meaningful and sufficient |
| **Activist Investors** | Earnings quality; accounting aggressiveness; hidden risk; misalignment between economic reality and reported figures |
| **Sector Regulators** | Industry-specific requirements and solvency/safety concerns (see selection logic below) |

**Sector Regulator selection:**
- Health insurance → NAIC, state insurance departments, CMS, HHS OIG
- Banking/financial services → OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve, CFPB
- Public companies (all sectors) → SEC (MD&A, non-GAAP, materiality), PCAOB
- Healthcare providers → CMS, HHS OIG, state health departments
- Investment management → SEC, FINRA
- When sector is unclear, default to SEC + the most relevant sector body

## Smart Selection — Which Segments to Run

**Core Audit** runs on every question, always. All eight roles.

**External stakeholders** always run. Select sector regulators based on the industry context of the question.

**IT Audit** runs when the topic involves any of:
- Automated controls or IT-dependent manual controls
- IT General Controls (ITGC) — access, change management, operations
- System-generated reports used as audit evidence
- Data integrity, completeness, or accuracy of financial data from systems
- ERP implementations, system migrations, or significant IT changes
- Cybersecurity risk with financial statement implications
- Data analytics as an audit procedure

**Tax** runs when the topic involves any of:
- Deferred tax assets or liabilities (ASC 740)
- Uncertain tax positions (FIN 48 / ASC 740-10)
- Tax-effected accounting positions where the book-tax difference is material
- Transfer pricing between related entities
- Tax reform impacts (TCJA, GILTI, FDII, BEAT, IRA 2022 corporate AMT)
- Significant tax attributes (NOL carryforwards, tax credits)
- Valuation allowance assessments

**Advisory** runs when the topic involves any of:
- Internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR) design or effectiveness
- Process design or re-engineering with financial reporting implications
- Enterprise risk management frameworks
- Areas where an advisory engagement is active or was recently completed for the same client
- Operational findings that have audit implications

State which segments you are running and why before proceeding.

## Stage 1 — Topic Analysis

Before generating perspectives, classify the audit question along two dimensions.

**Question type:**
- **Technical accounting** — GAAP/IFRS position, standard application, measurement methodology
- **Audit procedure** — how to gather sufficient appropriate evidence for a specific assertion
- **Risk assessment** — nature, likelihood, and magnitude of a financial statement misstatement risk
- **Control** — design adequacy, operating effectiveness, reliance decision, deficiency assessment
- **Quality / independence** — ethical requirements, quality management standards (ISQM 1/2, SQMS), scope
- **Regulatory / compliance** — what a rule, standard, or regulator requires and whether the engagement meets it

**Industry context:**
Name the client's industry and identify the governing regulatory bodies. This determines which sector regulators to include in the external perspective.

State both classifications, state which segments you are running, and then proceed.

---

## Stage 2 — The Perspective Grid

For each selected segment, write perspectives from the roles that have meaningfully distinct views.

**Rules:**

1. **Collapse roles with similar views.** If Senior and Staff see the question identically, write one combined "Staff / Senior" perspective. Never pad with empty distinctions — a perspective that sounds like another has failed.

2. **Write each role in its own register.** The register differences below are non-negotiable:
   - *Staff / Senior:* "What does this procedure actually look like on the ground? Do we have the evidence? Can we finish this in the time budgeted?"
   - *Manager:* "Is the overall approach adequate to address the risk? Can I defend the methodology in the manager review? Does the client have what we need?"
   - *Senior Manager / Executive Director:* "Is the scope calibrated to the risk? Is the fee adequate? Are we carrying risk we haven't priced?"
   - *Partner:* "What is the firm's exposure if this goes wrong? What does the client relationship require? Am I comfortable signing?"
   - *Independent Reviewer:* "Does this difficult position hold up under independent scrutiny? Would a reasonable, informed third party reach the same conclusion?"
   - *Quality Reviewer (EQCR):* "Would this file survive a PCAOB inspection or AICPA peer review? Does the documentation support every conclusion?"

3. **Each perspective: 3–5 sentences.** Specific. No hedging. Write as if that person is speaking directly. What does this role see that the others structurally cannot?

4. For **IT Audit, Tax, and Advisory**, apply the same hierarchy gradient: Staff/Senior focus on execution and technical detail; Manager focuses on approach design and client interaction; Senior Manager/ED focus on risk calibration and cross-engagement consistency; Partner focuses on firm risk and sign-off comfort.

5. For **External stakeholders**, write each voice as if they are reviewing the finished audit file or financial statements from the outside. They do not have access to management representations or engagement discussions — only what is documented.

---

## Stage 3 — The Tension Map

Map where perspectives actively conflict. Output a table, not prose.

For each tension:

| Column | What to write |
|---|---|
| Parties | Who disagrees with whom (role, segment, or internal vs. external) |
| What it's about | The precise point of disagreement — not "they see it differently" but what fact, interpretation, or value is in dispute |
| Type | **Operational** (how to execute), **Risk** (how much risk), **Regulatory** (what rules require), or **Incentive-driven** (who benefits from each position) |
| Load-bearing? | Yes / No — the load-bearing tension is the one where resolving it settles the most downstream questions |

**Tension categories to always check:**

- **Hierarchy tension within Core Audit:** Staff sees an execution problem; Partner doesn't know it exists. This is where engagement risk concentrates.
- **Core Audit vs. IT Audit:** Reliance on automated controls vs. substantive testing approach — different views on what's efficient and what's sufficient.
- **Core Audit vs. Tax:** A technical accounting position that is GAAP-supportable but creates an IRS exposure, or vice versa.
- **Core Audit vs. Quality Reviewer:** The engagement team is comfortable with the position; the EQCR is not.
- **Internal team vs. PCAOB Inspection:** What the team documented and what would survive an AS 2301 or AS 2201 deficiency finding.
- **Management / client vs. all audit segments:** Where client pressure conflicts with what the audit requires.

Flag the load-bearing tension explicitly.

---

## Stage 4 — Synthesis

Structure the output as follows:

```
# [Topic]: The Complete Audit View

## What the full team agrees on
## Where the team divides — and why
## The regulatory and inspection exposure
## The load-bearing question
## Practical recommendation
```

**Rules:**

- **The practical recommendation must be specific enough to act on.** Not "exercise judgment" — name the next step, who owns it, and what evidence or documentation it requires.
- **Keep the PCAOB / regulatory lens visible throughout.** Every conclusion should answer implicitly: would this survive inspection?
- **Name the hierarchy tension explicitly if it is load-bearing.** The gap between what Staff knows on the ground and what Partner has documented is one of the most common sources of audit deficiencies in practice.
- **Preserve dissent that survived Stage 3.** A synthesis that erases every disagreement has overfit to the majority view. If the EQCR and the Partner are genuinely divided, the output should say so — not paper over it.
- **Sector-specific regulators get the last word on the regulatory exposure section.** Their criteria are often stricter or differently framed than GAAP/GAAS, and a file that satisfies GAAS may still create a regulatory finding.
- **End on what an audit manager or senior manager should do next.** Research that doesn't connect to a decision is trivia.

---

## Quality bar

This worked if the output contains at least one thing that would not appear in a single-perspective answer:

- A role-specific constraint that changes the execution approach (e.g., the Quality Reviewer's inspection-readiness lens forcing a documentation step that the Manager would have skipped)
- A cross-segment conflict the engagement team hadn't surfaced (e.g., IT Audit's concern about the system-generated report the Core Audit team is relying on)
- A regulatory exposure visible only from the external lens (e.g., NAIC solvency concern that makes a technically GAAP-compliant position problematic for a health insurer)
- A hierarchy tension that reveals where the real decision sits (e.g., the Staff auditor has a scope concern they haven't escalated because the budget is already tight)

If everything in the synthesis is the conventional audit textbook view restated more thoroughly, the method didn't run — go back to Stage 2 and sharpen the voices.

## Example topics this works for

- "How should we approach the risk assessment for IBNR reserves at a health insurer?"
- "What is the right level of reliance on automated controls in a SAP environment?"
- "The client wants to capitalize a cost that we believe should be expensed — how does this look across the engagement?"
- "How do we assess the valuation allowance on a $500M deferred tax asset?"
- "What are the audit implications of a mid-year reinsurance treaty with an affiliate captive?"
- "How should we document our conclusion on a going concern assessment?"

## Provenance

This skill adapts Stanford OVAL Lab's STORM method (Shao et al., NAACL 2024) — multi-perspective question asking as a research technique — into an audit-specific framework. The segment structure and hierarchy gradient are original. The four-stage flow (classify → perspectives → tensions → synthesis) mirrors STORM's architecture. The external stakeholder layer (PCAOB, AICPA, regulators) reconstructs the "adversarial reviewer" perspective that STORM simulates through academic citation.
